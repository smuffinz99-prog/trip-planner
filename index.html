Paperwork & Visas

Great news for Australian passport holders — both Malaysia and Japan are visa-free for tourism. No applications, no fees. Still plenty to organise though.

🇲🇾 Malaysia — VISA FREE
90 days visa-free entry for Australians. No application needed. Just have a valid passport, onward/return ticket, and proof of funds. Fill the MDAC (Malaysia Digital Arrival Card) online within 3 days of arrival.
🇯🇵 Japan — VISA FREE
90 days visa-free entry for Australians. No application needed. Pre-register on Visit Japan Web for fast-track immigration & customs QR codes.

One bag, one coat

Packing

Pack ultralight. Malaysia is hot and humid (28–33°C), Japan in October is mild (13–21°C). Layer with the travel coat and buy cheap clothes locally.

🧥 The Travel Coat Strategy
A SCOTTeVEST or BauBax-style travel coat with 15+ pockets carries your electronics, documents, water bottle and snacks — and counts as a personal item / "worn item" on most airlines, freeing your 7kg carry-on. Wear it through security, not in the bag.

🇸🇬 Free bonus stop

Singapore — Layover Day

Your Scoot flight home already connects through Singapore. Book a Scoot itinerary with a long daytime layover and you get a full day in Singapore for no extra flight and no hotel — just walk out of Changi, explore, and fly out that evening.

✈️ How it works (the cheap trick)
On the Tokyo → Singapore → Brisbane Scoot route, pick the connection with a ~8–12h daytime gap in Singapore. Same single ticket, so no added airfare. You're carry-on only, so you just walk out of Changi with your backpack — no baggage carousel, no checked-bag worries.
😴 No hotel needed
A daytime layover means you never sleep in Singapore. If your only option runs overnight, Changi Airport itself has free 24h rest areas (and is genuinely a sight — see the Jewel waterfall) — so still no hotel cost. Aim for the daytime gap though.
⚠️ Do before you fly
Australians are visa-free for Singapore, but you MUST submit the free SG Arrival Card (SGAC) online within 3 days before arrival — even for a layover where you leave the airport. Have your onward Brisbane ticket handy (you do).

How money works on the road

Currency — How & When

You earn and budget in AUD, but you'll spend in Ringgit (MYR) and Yen (JPY). Here's exactly how to convert, when to do it, and how to dodge the bad-rate traps. Every booking link on this site is already set to show prices in AUD.

💡 The golden rule
Never exchange cash at home or at airport money-changers — their rates are 5–10% worse. Instead, carry a Wise/Revolut card and withdraw local cash from real bank ATMs as you go. You get the near-perfect mid-market rate automatically.

🗓️ When to do what

  • 2–3 weeks before: Order a Wise &/or Revolut card. Load it with AUD in the app. Free, arrives by post; the app converts AUD→MYR/JPY at the real rate when you spend
  • Before you fly: Get ~$100 AUD in a mix as untouched emergency cash. Don't bulk-buy MYR/JPY at home. Notify your bank of travel dates so cards aren't frozen
  • Land in KL (Oct 13): Walk PAST the airport forex. Withdraw ~RM300–400 from a Maybank/CIMB ATM in arrivals. Pay by Wise card where accepted; keep cash for hawkers & Grab
  • Land in Osaka (Oct 28): Withdraw ¥20,000–30,000 from a 7-Eleven ATM (they accept foreign cards; most bank ATMs don't). Japan is cash-heavy — always carry yen
  • Throughout: Withdraw smaller amounts a few times rather than one big lump. Less to lose if a wallet goes missing; check the app for each withdrawal fee
  • At every card terminal: If asked "pay in AUD or local currency?" — always choose LOCAL (MYR/JPY). Choosing AUD triggers "dynamic currency conversion" — a hidden ~5% markup
  • Before flying home: Spend down leftover cash (snacks, gifts at 100-yen shops). Coins can't be changed back — only notes

☪️ Eat well, eat halal

Food & Halal Guide

One of you eats strictly halal — no exceptions. Malaysia makes that effortless; Japan needs planning. Every meal flagged ☪️ in the itineraries is a verified halal or seafood/veg-safe option for that traveller.

☪️ Strict-halal rule of thumb: in Japan, assume broth, sauces & dressings contain pork or alcohol (mirin/sake) unless certified otherwise. Stick to certified-halal spots (Sukiya, Muslim/Indian restaurants), confirmed seafood/veg, or sealed conbini items with checked labels. When unsure — skip it. The other traveller eats anything, so split orders freely.

🇲🇾 Malaysia — Halal Heaven

Almost everything is halal. Look for the official JAKIM halal logo. Muslim-friendly by default.

  • Nasi lemak — RM2–5 · national dish
  • Satay — grilled skewers, peanut sauce
  • Mee goreng — RM5 · fried noodles
  • Rendang — slow-cooked beef
  • Roti canai — RM2 · with dhal/curry
  • Teh tarik — RM1.50 · pulled milk tea
The non-halal friend can find pork & Chinese dishes easily at Chinese food courts and kopitiams.

🇯🇵 Japan — Plan Ahead

Halal needs research — pork & alcohol (mirin, sake) hide in many sauces & broths. Reliable options:

  • Sukiya — certified halal beef bowls ☪️
  • Conveyor sushi — ask for no pork, stick to fish
  • Indian/Muslim restaurants in Asakusa & Shinjuku
  • Conbini egg & tuna onigiri (check labels)
  • Tokyo Camii mosque, Yoyogi — halal dining nearby
Apps: HalalTrip & Halal Gourmet Japan map verified restaurants nearby.
🍢 Cheap & easy wins
In Malaysia, hawker markets (Jalan Alor, Gurney Drive) are the move — fresh, halal, RM5–12 a plate. In Japan, 7-Eleven & FamilyMart are lifesavers: onigiri, salads, fruit, hot drinks, all cheap and quick.
